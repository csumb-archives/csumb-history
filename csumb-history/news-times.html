  • January 1, 1994 - The North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA) goes into effect.

  • January 6 - Nancy Kerrigan is clubbed on the leg by an assailant under orders from figure skating rival Tonya Harding.

  • January 11 - The Irish government announces the end of a 20-year broadcasting ban on the Irish Republican Army (IRA) and its political arm, Sinn Fein.

  • January 14 - U.S. President Bill Clinton and Russian President Boris Yeltsin sign the Kremlin Accords, which stop the preprogrammed aiming of nuclear missiles at targets, and also provide for the dismantling of the nuclear arsenal in the Ukraine.

  • January 17 - The Northridge Earthquake, of magnitude 6.7, hits the San Fernando Valley of Los Angeles at 4:31 AM.

  • February 3 - William J. Perry is sworn in as the 19th Secretary of Defense of the United States.

  • February 5 - Byron De La Beckwith is convicted of the 1963 murder of civil rights leader Medgar Evers.

  • February 6 - A Serb mortar shell kills 68 and wounds about 200 in a Sarajevo marketplace.

  • February 9 - The Vance-Owen peace plan for Bosnia and Herzegovina is announced.

  • February 22 - United States CIA agent Aldrich Ames and his wife are charged with spying for the Soviet Union by the United States Department of Justice. Ames will later be convicted and sentenced to life imprisonment. His wife will receive a sentence of 5 years in prison.

  • February 25 - Jewish settler Baruch Goldstein opens fire inside the Tomb of the Patriarchs in the West Bank. He kills 29 Muslims before worshipers beat him to death.

  • February 28 - U.S. warplanes shoot down four Serb aircraft over Bosnia, in the first NATO use of force in the troubled area.

  • March 4 - Four terrorists are convicted for their roles in the 1993 World Trade Center bombing, which killed six and injured more than a thousand.

  • March 5 - Arizona law firm Canter & Siegel "spams" the internet with email advertising green-card lottery services. Net citizens flame back. To many people, this event, coming not long after the National Science Foundation lifted its unofficial ban on commercial speech on the internet, marks the end of the internet's early period, when the original "Netiquette" could still be enforced.

  • March 12 - The Church of England ordains its first female priests.

  • March 23 - "Shock jock" Howard Stern formally announces his candidacy for the New York governorship, on the Libertarian Party ticket.

  • March 24 - At an election rally in Tijuana, Mexican presidential candidate Luis Donaldo Colosio is assassinated. Mario Aburto Martinez is arrested for the crime and confesses on the same day.

  • March 28 - In South Africa, Zulus and African National Congress supporters battle in central Johannesburg. Eighteen people are killed.

  • March 31 - The journal Nature reports the finding in Ethiopia of the first complete Australopithecus afarensis skull.

  • April 6 - Rwandan president Juvénal Habyarimana and president of Burundi Cyprien Ntaryamira die when a missile shoots down their jet near Kigali, Rwanda. Hours later, the Rwandan genocide begins. Some U.N. camps shelter civilians, but most of the U.N. peacekeeping forces stand by while the slaughter goes on-they are forbidden to intervene because this would breach their "monitoring" mandate. Over 100 days, ten percent of Rwanda's population-800,000 Tutsis and moderate Hutus-will be killed by their countrymen, often with machetes.

  • April 8 - Kurt Cobain, lead singer of Nirvana, is found dead in Seattle, Washington. He had committed suicide three days earlier.

  • April 21 - The U.N. Security Council votes unanimously to withdraw most of its peacekeeping troops from Rwanda, cutting the force from 2,500 to 270. The Red Cross reports that 100,000 Tutsis may have been killed in the last two weeks.

  • April 26 - South Africa holds its first fully multiracial elections.

  • April 28 - U.S. State Department spokeswoman Christine Shelley is asked whether what is happening in Rwanda is a genocide. She responds, ". . . the use of the term 'genocide' has a very precise legal meaning, although it's not strictly a legal determination. There are other factors in there as well." However, a secret intelligence report by the State Department issued as early as the end of April calls the killings genocide. Two days later, the U.N. Security Council passes a resolution condemning the killing, but omits the word "genocide." If the term is used, the U.N. will be legally obliged to act to "prevent and punish" the perpetrators.

  • May 6 - The Channel Tunnel, which took 15,000 workers over seven years to complete, opens between England and France. Vehicles can now travel between the two countries in 35 minutes. The tunnel initially opens with a reduced freight service, and will open to passenger vehicles in December.

  • May 9 - Nelson Mandela is inaugurated as South Africa's first black president.

  • June 12 - Nicole Brown Simpson and Ronald Goldman are murdered outside her home in Los Angeles, California. O.J. Simpson is later acquitted of the killings, but is held liable in a civil suit.

  • June 15 - The Lion King, as of 2004 the third-highest-grossing animated film of all time, opens in theaters nationwide.

  • June 17 - NFL star O.J. Simpson and his friend Al Cowlings flee from police in his white Ford Bronco. The low-speed chase, which unfolds live on television, ends at Simpson's mansion in Brentwood, California, where he then surrenders to police.

  • July, 1994 - The planet Jupiter is pelted by 21 large fragments of Comet Shoemaker-Levy 9 over the course of 6 days.

  • July 7 - Aden is occupied by troops from North Yemen.

  • July 18 - In Buenos Aires, an explosion destroys a building housing several Jewish organizations, killing 96 and injuring many more.

  • July 25 - Israel and Jordan sign the Washington Declaration, which formally ends the state of war that has existed between the nations since 1948.

  • September 3 - Russia and the People's Republic of China agree to cease targeting their nuclear weapons against each other.

  • September 8 - USAir Flight 427, with 132 people on board, crashes on approach to Pittsburgh International Airport. There are no survivors.

  • September 28 - The car ferry MS Estonia sinks in the Baltic Sea, killing 852 people.

  • September-October - Iraq threatens to stop cooperating with UNSCOM inspectors and begins once again to deploy troops near its border with Kuwait. In response, the U.S. begins to deploy troops to Kuwait.

  • October 8 - The president of the U.N. Security Council says that Iraq must withdraw its troops from the Kuwaiti border and immediately cooperate with weapons inspectors.

  • October 12 - NASA loses radio contact with the Magellan spacecraft as the probe descends into the thick atmosphere of Venus. It is believed that the spacecraft burned up in the atmosphere.

  • October 15 - After three years of exile in the U.S., Haiti's president Jean-Bertrand Aristide returns to his country.

  • October 26 - Jordan and Israel sign a peace treaty.

  • October 31 - An American Eagle ATR-72 crashes in Roselawn, Indiana, after circling in icy weather, killing 64 passengers.

  • October 31 - HRH The Duke of Edinburgh attends a ceremony in Israel where his late mother, HSH Princess Alice of Battenberg, is honored as "Righteous among the Nations" for sheltering Jewish families from the Nazis in Athens during World War II.

  • November 3 - Red Hat Linux 1.0 is released.

  • November 5 - A letter by former U.S. President Ronald Reagan is released, announcing that he has Alzheimer's disease.

  • November 8 - Georgia Representative Newt Gingrich leads the United States Republican Party in taking control of both the House of Representatives and the Senate in midterm congressional elections, the first time in 40 years that the Republicans have secured control of both houses of Congress.

  • November 13 - The first passengers travel through the Channel Tunnel between England and France.

  • November 16 - A federal judge issues a temporary restraining order that prohibits the State of California from implementing Proposition 187, which would deny most public services to illegal aliens.

  • November 20 - The Angolan government and UNITA rebels sign the Lusaka Protocol in Zambia, ending 19 years of civil war. (In 1995, localized fighting will resume.)

  • November 28 - Voters in Norway reject European Union membership.

  • November 30 - The famous hip-hop artist Tupac Shakur survives five bullets in an apparent robbery attempt outside a New York music studio.

  • December 2 - The Australian government agrees to pay reparations to the Aboriginal people who were displaced during the nuclear tests in 1950s and 1960s.

  • December 11 - Boris Yeltsin orders troops into Chechnya.

  • December 11 - A small bomb explodes on Philippine Airlines Flight 434, killing a Japanese businessman. The bombing is a field test done by Ramzi Yousef to test explosives planned for use in Project Bojinka, a terrorist attack plan that will be exposed after an apartment fire.

  • December 19 - A planned exchange rate correction of the Mexican Peso to the U.S. Dollar becomes a massive financial meltdown in Mexico, unleashing what would later be widely called the 'Tequila effect' on global financial markets. This will prompt a $50 billion 'bailout' by the U.S. Clinton administration.

  • December 19 - The Whitewater Scandal investigation begins.

  • December 26 - French anti-terrorist police storm a hijacked jet in Marseille and kill 4 Islamist terrorists.

  • December 31 - The total number of computers hooked to the internet is estimated at 2,864,000.

  • December 31 - The total number of internet users by the end of the year tops 14 million.

  • December 31, 1994 - The number of countries reachable by email is now 140.

More internet News 1994

  • Commercialisation of the internet begins: Shopping malls arrive on the internet; First Virtual, the first cyberbank, opens for business; the first flower shop (Grant's Florist) begins taking orders via the internet; the first banner ads appear on hotwired.com.
  • Communities begin to be wired up directly to the internet (Lexington and Cambridge, Massachusetts, USA). The U.S. Senate and House bring information servers online.

Prizes 1994

  • Pulitzer Prize for Fiction: E. Annie Proulx, The Shipping News
  • Pulitzer Prize for Poetry: Yusef Komunyakaa, Neon Vernacular: New and Selected Poems
  • Nobel Prize for Literature: Kenzaburo Oe
