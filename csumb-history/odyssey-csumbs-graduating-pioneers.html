--- layout: history title: The Odyssey of CSUMB's Graduating "Pioneers" ---

According to Webster's Dictionary, a pioneer is "a person who originates a new line of thought or activity; someone who starts or leads others in the development of something new or anyone who ventures into the unknown."

The 33 Pioneers did just that four years ago. CSUMB graduates 291 seniors this year, 33 of whom are "Pioneer" students. (To be considered a "Pioneer," the student must have started his or her college career as a freshman at CSUMB in August of 1995, when CSUMB opened for its first semester.)

When the school opened in 1995, students found out just how "new" this school really was: they moved into the lone residence hall only hours after the last bit of dust had been swept up&#8212and, they say, while the paint was still drying. Their first CSUMB meals had to be catered because there was, as yet, no on-campus grill. The pioneers persevered, often having to travel off campus to attend their first CSUMB classes at a local elementary school (until CSUMB's classrooms were finished a few weeks into the semester).

CSUMB's opening was full of firsts. Pioneers were the first students ever to have an otter as a mascot; they were also the first in the nation to live on a vacant army base as students. The day of orientation, the bookstore opened with the first set of brand new books and supplies. The first CSUMB T-shirts and sweatshirts were swept off the shelves as enthusiastic students and their parents searched for the CSUMB identity they would help to create. New email accounts were assigned to CSUMB's first 654 students.

The day after they arrived, the Pioneers headed northward to attend the first-ever Otter Days, hosted by Outward Bound in the Santa Cruz Mountains. Several days later, on September 5, 1995, they came back to the inauguration of the new university with President Bill Clinton as guest of honor at the opening of the 21st CSU campus. The pioneers led their fellow Otters by participating in every aspect of the university, including sitting on committees to give input on everything from graduation requirements to landscaping. They even participated in search committees responsible for choosing faculty members for the future.
