--- layout: history title: National Ocean Conference in Monterey ---

Recognizing that the ocean is vital to life on earth, the United Nations declares 1998 as the International Year of the Ocean. The National Ocean Conference, held on June 12 in Monterey, plays an important role in the year and, in turn, CSUMB faculty and students play an important role in the conference.

Faculty and students from Earth Systems Science & Policy (ESSP) and the Spatial Information and Visualization & Analysis (SIVA) Resources Center take their labs on the road on June 12, speaking on science issues at the Oceans Fair at Custom House Plaza in Monterey. The ESSP exhibit is housed in the Marine Advanced Technology Education (MATE) booth. (The MATE program is a collaborative effort between Monterey Peninsula College, CSUMB, and local industries.)

Steve Moore, ESSP Assistant Professor, hosts an exhibit featuring Moore's Acoustic Doppler Velocimeter, which illustrates the measurement of fluid motion. The instrumentation used in the measurement is demonstrated in an aquarium while the recorded measurements are displayed on a computer screen. Students from CSUMB's ESSP 330 course, Electronic Projects for Environmental Measurement, conduct similar experiments using instrumentation with sensors and circuitry. Additional CSUMB exhibitors include ESSP Associate Professor Rikk Kvitek and Jack Paris, SIVA Resources Center Director. Kvitek and two of his students share information through an exhibit anchored by an acoustic seafloor-mapping system which students use to provide critical habitat information to the Department of California Fish and Game. Paris orchestrates the third exhibit, which deals with satellite pictures and map data. Despite being a relatively new program in the university's science family, ESSP currently boasts 250 majors.

"What ESSP is all about is finding viable solutions to real environmental issues," says Moore. "Traditionally, there's been a real disconnect between science and policy. Often, the missing link was economics. Our goal in this major is to train students to speak both languages: science and economics, and how policy is made."
