--- layout: history title: Community Day of Learning Focuses on University Learning Requirements ---

On January 25, Community Day of Learning follows President Peter Smith's State of the University address. One of the workshops for Community Day of Learning is designed to focus on the continued evolution of CSUMB's University Learning Requirements (ULRs).

CSUMB has established learning requirements that are different than those at most universities, and the university put the ULRs through a review in preparation for last October's visit by an accreditation team from the Western Association of Schools and Colleges (WASC). (Unlike most universities that graduate students based on which courses have been taken, CSUMB graduation is based on what a student has learned.)

According to CSUMB's ULR Website: "Our system works a little bit like getting your driver's license. To get a license, you need to demonstrate that you know how to drive and that you know the rules of the road. You can learn these things in a variety of ways, for example, taking private lessons, doing trial and error, or some combination. When you do get your license, you are not held accountable for how you learned to drive, but rather for demonstrating that you are able to drive."

The ULR workshop presents an overview of CSUMB's ULRs, after which the group breaks into smaller groups&#8212each focusing on an individual ULR. The workshops are intended to help faculty and staff understand the criteria and protocols for each ULR through mediated discussions. Most universities throughout the country, on average, have only five ULRs (also know as general education outcomes or requirements), but in order to meet the goals of CSUMB's Vision and Mission, the university needed several more.

"We are part of a whole new movement in higher education that is trying to make general education have a more specific direction," explains Amy Driscoll, director of Teaching, Learning, and Assessment.

CSUMB's ULRs are: Community Participation; Creative/Artistic Expression; Culture and Equity; Democratic Participation; English Communication; Ethics; Language; Literature/Popular Culture; Mathematics Communication; Science, Technology and Information; United States Histories; and Vibrancy.
