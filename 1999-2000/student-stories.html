Amy Lauder

Then: Amy Lauder is a 2000 graduate from CSUMB in Liberal Studies, with a Recreation emphasis. Originally from Orange County, Amy came north in January 1997-an upper-division transfer from a community college in southern California. She says she came to CSUMB "because it didn't have athletic scholarships [in 1997] and didn't have a Greek system; and because they did have big housing, so I didn't have to live in the dorms; I could live in an apartment." During her time at CSUMB, her accomplishments and activities included:

  • Resident Advisor (RA) on campus for two years
  • First paid student-instructor for Otter Days
  • Worked for SOAR as a Peer Advisor

Lauder says: "I was originally a Collaborative Health and Human Services [CHHS] major with a Parks & Recreation emphasis, but I was the only student in the program. So I had to switch majors and eventually majored in Liberal Studies with an emphasis in Parks & Recreation. Brian Simmons [CHHS faculty member] helped me find the right major and stays in contact with me today."

Now: After graduating and taking a trip to Europe, Amy started worked at CSUMB in University Advancement-the same department in which she'd been a student assistant. She was the Events & Alumni Assistant for over three years, before moving back to Southern California. Lauder saiys: "I want to get back into recreation-day camps, after-school programs-and eventually go to cooking school.

Among Amy's most vivid CSUMB memories is a challenging class that helped bring some interesting perspectives, one that likely wouldn't be offered anywhere other than CSUMB: "Relating to Whiteness."

"It was about how your 'whiteness' oppressed others. [Instructor] Debian Marty was great-she ran the class wonderfully. And that's a class that could easily get out of control. A lot of stuff came out of it, and it has the ability to [get out of control]. Coming out of the class you think the world much worse than it really is. Five years later, I realize that it's not as bad as you think it is."

Rafael Zamora

Then: Rafael Zamora was a Management & International Entrepreneurship (MIE) major, and graduated from CSUMB in 1996. His accomplishments and activities at CSUMB included:

  • Member of the first graduating class
  • One of the first members of the CSUMB President's Council
  • Athletics Chair on CSUMB Alumni Association Board of Directors

Transferring to CSUMB as a junior from a community college in Stockton, the Hawaiian-born Zamora-an avid scuba-diver-says he was "intrigued" when he heard of a university opening up in the Monterey Bay area.

"The faculty [members] were influential. Jeff Froshman [now a lecturer for the School of Business] really helped me find my way. I have fond memories of one class--'Hot Topics in Entrepreneurship.' We did independent research on topics affecting entrepreneurs. We had to present [our findings] in class. That really helped me with what I'm doing today; it gave me a very strong structure."

Zamora continues: "The university became my laboratory in my quest for knowledge. I learned the fundamentals of computers, Japanese, and business because I was given the freedom to design my own program: a Pacific Rim and technology focus. CSUMB's emphasis on self-responsibility was excellent preparation for transitioning from academia to corporate America."

During his time at CSUMB, Zamora took an internship with Sony's Information Technologies Division. The position armed him with hot new products, like the video camera-equipped subnotebook that wowed colleagues on the CSUMB President's Council. A member of the very first CSUMB graduating class, Zamora applied the same energy and insight in his role as Athletics Chair on the CSUMB Alumni Association Board of Directors.

Now: After four years with Sony (his internship later led to a full-time position), Zamora has worked with Dell as a Systems Consultant since 2000, based in the San Francisco Bay Area. "I'm a business consultant; I go to where the customer is. I'm headed to Chicago, then to Boston (at the time of the 2004 Democratic National Convention). When a company wants to talk about how technology affects their bottom line, I'm the guy who represents Dell."
