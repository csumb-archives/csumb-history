--- layout: history title: Second Wave Faculty Member Speaks Out ---

Rina Benmayor arrives as part of the "second wave" faculty in 1995 from Hunter College in New York. ("It was very appealing-getting in on the ground floor and building something designed from scratch.") After originally being hired to be part of Service Learning, she moves to SBS (Social & Behavioral Sciences) and eventually joins Josina Makau in Human Communication. (She's been there ever since, currently overseeing the Oral History Archives, in addition to her teaching duties.)

About the university's earliest days: "We had to learn how to do budgets, run a department while teaching, design majors and outcomes. It was a real challenge, but it was creative. We could sit around and have it be a collective effort. It was great to be able to design a program that wasn't inherited, where people could bring different ideas to the table." (It's apparently worked very well at HCOM: six of the eight President's Award winners have been HCOM students.)

"We've had incredible students. I remember Jose Aguilera; we called him 'Augie.' He came here, and he was someone with a huge thirst for knowledge. He was a local boy [from Salinas]. There have been so many students like him, but to see his evolution . . . He became very active in student organizations. He lapped up what he was learning here. When he got the President's Award, he got up at the graduation [ceremony] and he asked his parents to stand up-it was the first graduation in the stadium-and he gave this incredible speech honoring his parents. Saying, 'you are graduating with me today. You're the reason I'm here.' It was such an impassioned speech. And to see that happen . . . He got a lot of support here and ran with it." (Jose Aguilera went on to graduate school at San Jose State University.)

About one of her first classes: "We were in a vault in Building 86. Richard Bains was teaching a music class right outside our door. We couldn't close the door [of the vault] because of the ventilation, and we couldn't keep the door open because of the music. It was a challenge."

About Troy Challenger, CSUMB alumnus and currently on campus as a Faculty Technology Consultant: "He was a student here [and] was in the first graduating class. He produced a Powerpoint presentation of the oral histories that all the students were doing for class. That was his Capstone." Dr. Benmayor gives Troy special mention for assisting in building the Oral History Archive, teaching faculty how to use software, helping with multiple audio-visual projects, helping with much of the infrastructure of the Library Learning Complex, the technology related to Distance Learning, and for giving numerous workshops across campus.

"[As a campus employee] he makes so much happen for so many people," Benmayor says. "We've always had a need for him here. We started to integrate technology into the classroom. That allowed us to encourage students to go online and start their own web pages. Troy brought people from the Digital Story community from Berkeley to teach us about it. That was absolutely wonderful. Troy is the person who would make it all happen. He's a saint. He's there to help-and he'll help anybody and everybody. I could never leave [CSUMB] because I could never find Troy anywhere else."

NOTE: For a sample of the Oral History Archive, visit a web page from a recent HCOM Oral History class.

About working at CSUMB: "People from other CSUs come here and say: 'You guys work harder than anywhere else.' How does that happen? We were trying to do something different, and we had a lot of turnover at the administrative level, a lot of tensions. The leadership on the day to day . . . people were being shuffled around; it makes for a lot of work at the lower levels. And some of the people who came here were a little nutty and wanted to do something different. So that meant that we had a different kind of expectation of ourselves."
